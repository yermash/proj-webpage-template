Stylistic Shaders in Unity

Uri Kreindler, Gabriel Mitnick, Brandon Suen, and Brandon Yermash

We will create a number of different non-photorealistic shaders in Unity and combine them into a demo scene. We will potentially create a single shader that is more complex and detailed to showcase the scene.

Question for TA’s: Would it be okay if we used Unity’s visual programming “shader graph” tool? Or should we use only Cg/HLSL code? Or a mix of the two?

Problem Description

Just because a renderer has optimal physically accurate doesn’t mean that it’s optimal for a specific application. In animations, static art, and interactive art like videogames, non-physically accurate shaders might give more desirable results. How can we go beyond the aesthetic value of physically-based rendering by making more stylized, unrealistic algorithms? We’ll approach this intersection of computer science and art by writing shaders for Unity, using Cg/HLSL and Unity’s shader graph tool. We’ll also create a sample scene in Unity to demonstrate the shaders.

Goals and Deliverables

We’ll make a simple demo scene in Unity with different types of geometry and some simple interaction. Our deliverables will be a playable Unity project and a video demo.

Our metric for quality will be a combination of subjective aesthetic judgment, visual legibility of objects, and rendering framerate. We want to come up with shaders that look nice, but that also allow a viewer to clearly understand the geometry shown. Also, we would like to be able to render a scene in real time at 30 or 60 fps for interactive applications.

Baseline plan: We plan to come up with a few different stylized shaders and at least a simple static scene to render with our shaders. As inspiration, we’re interested in replicating different cel shading styles from games like Breath of the Wild and Borderlands, but also playing with the code to come up with our own new approaches.

Stretch goal: We would like to expand on the scene and make it interactive; and combine the different styles we find into a more complex shader to render different objects with algorithms representing different materials. Our north star here is a game currently under development on a YouTube channel called t3ssel8r. It has an awesome retro pixel-art style that looks like hand-drawn 2D sprites, but is actually rendered from 3D geometry. There’s special handling of different objects like grass and water. We’d love to figure out how this is done and replicate it or make our own style.
